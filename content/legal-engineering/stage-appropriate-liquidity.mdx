The original venture capital model assumed a fairly simple lifecycle. A fund raises capital, deploys it over two or three years, and returns it over the following five to seven. Ten years, start to finish. The math was tight, the timelines were reasonable, and the illiquidity premium made sense because the illiquidity was temporary.

That model no longer describes reality. More than 40 percent of today's unicorns raised their first round over a decade ago. Companies stay private not for five or seven years but for thirteen or more, accumulating enormous value while their investors, employees, and early backers wait for a liquidity event that may never come in the form originally anticipated. The venture model's core financial contract — you give me your money, I'll give it back in ten years with a multiple — has quietly broken.

## The Market Already Solved This

The private secondary market exists because the primary model stopped working. In 2024, secondary transaction volume hit an all-time high of $152 to $160 billion, up roughly 39 percent from 2023, and projections suggest it will exceed $210 billion in 2025 and reach $300 billion by 2030. These are not fringe transactions. Venture secondaries now represent 29 percent of all venture deal activity, up from 16 percent in 2020. In 2024, a remarkable 71 percent of all venture exits occurred through the secondary market, not through IPOs or acquisitions.

The numbers tell a clear story. Between July 2024 and June 2025, VC secondary volume reached $61.1 billion — actually surpassing the $58.8 billion in VC-backed IPO value over the same period. The secondary market is no longer a sideshow. For many venture investors, it has become the primary exit path.

Major companies have recognized this shift and responded with structured tender offers. Canva ran a tender exceeding $1 billion. Stripe facilitated transactions at a $70 billion valuation. Databricks conducted a $10 billion tender at $62 billion. These are sophisticated, well-capitalized companies deliberately creating liquidity channels for their stakeholders because the traditional exit timeline has extended beyond what anyone originally agreed to.

## Friction Is Not Protection

Here is where the regulatory framework falls behind the market. Despite the explosive growth in secondary transactions, the legal mechanics of executing these trades remain needlessly complex. Each transaction implicates securities transfer restrictions, right-of-first-refusal provisions, information asymmetry concerns, and a patchwork of exemptions that were designed for a different era of private markets.

The result is that secondary transactions are expensive to execute, slow to close, and concentrated among sophisticated players who can absorb the legal costs. In 2024, 61 percent of secondary trading value came from just 15 companies. That concentration is partly a function of demand, but it is also a function of transaction costs that make smaller deals uneconomical. An LP in a venture fund who wants to sell a $500,000 position faces many of the same legal hurdles as one selling $50 million, without the same resources to navigate them.

There is a common instinct in securities regulation to treat friction as a feature — the assumption that if a transaction is hard to execute, investors are being protected. But friction in this context does not protect investors. It traps them. An LP who cannot access the secondary market is not safer. They are simply locked into a position they may need or want to exit, with no recourse except to wait and hope.

The market's growing maturity should provide regulators comfort, not concern. Median discounts on secondary transactions shrank from 46 percent in December 2023 to just 3 percent by the end of 2024. That compression reflects deep, efficient price discovery among informed participants. This is not a market characterized by information failures or predatory pricing. It is a market finding fair value, quickly and accurately, when the participants are allowed to transact.

## Three Changes That Would Matter

The regulatory reforms needed here are modest in scope but significant in impact. None of them require rethinking the fundamental framework of securities regulation. They require adapting existing rules to a market structure that has already emerged.

First, a safe harbor for limited resales among accredited investors. The current exemption framework was designed primarily for initial issuances, not for secondary transfers among sophisticated parties who already hold the securities. A clear safe harbor for resales among accredited investors — with reasonable volume limitations and holding period requirements — would remove the single largest source of friction in the secondary market without compromising investor protection. These are accredited investors selling to accredited investors. The paternalistic justification for heavy regulation simply does not apply.

Second, a non-integration clarification. Under current doctrine, multiple offerings can be "integrated" and treated as a single offering, potentially blowing up exemptions that each transaction independently qualifies for. This creates real risk for fund managers and companies that facilitate secondary transactions alongside primary fundraising. A clear statement that bona fide secondary transactions will not be integrated with primary offerings would reduce legal uncertainty and the associated advisory costs.

Third, streamlined disclosure requirements for small-lot transactions. The information asymmetry rationale for disclosure rules is strongest when there is a large gap between what the company knows and what the buyer knows. In a secondary transaction between two accredited investors, both of whom have access to the company's financials (or have priced in the lack of access), the marginal benefit of mandated disclosure is low relative to its cost. A simplified disclosure regime for transactions below a reasonable threshold — say, $1 million — would open the market to smaller LPs and employee shareholders who are currently priced out by legal fees.

## Aligning Regulation with Reality

I want to be precise about the claim I am making. I am not arguing that secondary markets should be unregulated, or that investor protection is unimportant. I am arguing that the current regulatory framework was designed for a world where companies went public in five to seven years and secondary transfers were rare exceptions. Neither of those assumptions holds anymore.

The market has adapted. Companies stay private longer. Secondary transactions have become the dominant exit mechanism. Price discovery among accredited investors is efficient and improving. The regulations have not adapted. They still treat every secondary transfer as a suspicious event requiring extensive legal engineering, when the evidence suggests these transactions are routine, well-priced, and desired by all parties.

At AngelList, I watched the secondary market evolve from an occasional convenience to a structural necessity. Fund managers need it to provide liquidity to LPs on a reasonable timeline. Employees need it to realize the value of their equity compensation before their companies are twenty years old. Investors need it to manage portfolio allocation across vintages. The demand is real, the participants are sophisticated, and the market is working. The regulatory framework should stop treating it as a problem and start treating it as infrastructure.
